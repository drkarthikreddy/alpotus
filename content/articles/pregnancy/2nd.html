Second Trimester Management: Comprehensive Clinical Protocols for 14-28 Weeks

Mastering the anatomy scan, metabolic screening, and secondary prevention of maternal-fetal complications.

The second trimester represents the "steady state" of pregnancy, yet it is clinically the most critical window for identifying structural anomalies and metabolic shifts. For the OBG specialist, this period is dominated by two massive pillars: the Level II Ultrasound (TIFFA) and the Gestational Diabetes (GDM) screen. A failure to optimize management during these 14 weeks can lead to irreversible obstetric catastrophes in the third trimester.

1. Maternal Physiology: Hemodynamic and Respiratory Shifts

Between weeks 14 and 20, cardiac output increases by 30-50%, primarily through an increase in stroke volume and later, heart rate. Systemic vascular resistance drops, leading to the characteristic second-trimester "dip" in blood pressure. Specialists must distinguish this physiological hypotension from pathological states. Furthermore, uterine expansion now reaches the level of the umbilicus by 20 weeks, shifting the center of gravity and increasing the risk of maternal falls and musculoskeletal strain.

The 20-Week Anomaly Scan (Level II Checklist)

  • CNS: Lateral ventricles (< 10mm), Cavum Septum Pellucidum (CSP), Cerebellum.
  • Face: Upper lip integrity (screen for cleft), Nose/Bone profile.
  • Cardiac: Four-chamber view, LVOT/RVOT (Outflow tracts), 3-vessel view.
  • Abdomen: Stomach bubble, Kidneys, Bladder, Cord insertion (3 vessels).
  • Spine: Longitudinal and transverse views for neural tube defects.
  • Placental Location: Distance from internal os (screen for Placenta Previa).

2. Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM): The Screening War

Insulin resistance peaks in the second trimester due to the secretion of Human Placental Lactogen (hPL). ACOG and the ADA recommend screening all pregnant women at 24–28 weeks. The OBG postgraduate must be proficient in the two standard strategies:

3. Preeclampsia Prophylaxis and Monitoring

Preeclampsia remains a leading cause of maternal morbidity. According to the USPSTF and ACOG, high-risk women (history of preeclampsia, multi-fetal gestation, or chronic hypertension) should have started Low-Dose Aspirin (81-150 mg) before 16 weeks. During the second trimester, clinicians should monitor the **Uterine Artery Doppler** at 20-24 weeks; high resistance or "notching" identifies patients at extreme risk for placental insufficiency and FGR (Fetal Growth Restriction).

Clinical Insight: Cervical Length (CL) Monitoring

Universal screening of cervical length at the 20-week scan is now a gold standard to prevent preterm birth. A CL < 25 mm before 24 weeks in a singleton pregnancy is the threshold for intervention. Management options for the specialist include Vaginal Progesterone (ACOG Level A) or Cervical Cerclage (McDonald or Shirodkar technique) if there is a history of prior spontaneous preterm birth.

4. Rh Isoimmunization: The Anti-D Protocol

For Rh-negative unsensitized women, the second trimester is the period of vigilance. While the standard 300mcg dose of **Anti-D Gamma Globulin** is administered at 28 weeks, the specialist must be ready to administer it earlier in cases of "sensitizing events" such as amniocentesis, abdominal trauma, or vaginal bleeding during the second trimester.

5. Nutrition: Iron and Calcium Kinetics

Fetal demand for iron increases exponentially during the 2nd trimester to support expanding fetal and maternal red cell mass.
Protocol: 30–60 mg of elemental iron and 1000 mg of Calcium daily. Note: Iron and Calcium should be taken 2 hours apart to prevent competitive inhibition of absorption—a common patient counseling error.

6. Immunization in the Second Trimester

The **Tdap vaccine** is ideally administered between 27 and 36 weeks to maximize passive antibody transfer to the fetus. However, the Influenza vaccine (inactivated) can and should be given at any point in the second trimester during flu season to prevent maternal respiratory complications.
