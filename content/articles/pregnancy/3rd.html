Third Trimester Management: Critical Surveillance and Intrapartum Optimization

Navigating the complexities of fetal growth, placental insufficiency, and the biomechanics of labor induction.

ADVERTISEMENT

The third trimester (28 weeks to delivery) is the clinical phase of "anticipatory management." For the OBG postgraduate, the focus shifts from anatomical screening to the rigorous assessment of fetal well-being and the physiological readiness of the mother for parturition. With fetal weight doubling in the final weeks, the metabolic and hemodynamic demands on the placenta are at their peak, making this the period of highest risk for stillbirth and preeclampsia.

1. Fetal Well-being: The Biophysical Profile (BPP)

Standard antepartum surveillance typically begins at 32-34 weeks for high-risk pregnancies. The specialist must master the Modified BPP, which combines the Non-Stress Test (NST) with the Amniotic Fluid Index (AFI). According to PubMed research (ID: PMC3263934), the BPP is an excellent predictor of fetal acid-base status.

2. Fetal Growth Restriction (FGR): Doppler Hemodynamics

Distinguishing between the "Small for Gestational Age" (SGA) fetus and true FGR is a core postgraduate competency. The Umbilical Artery (UA) Doppler is the primary surveillance tool. The loss of end-diastolic flow (AREDF) or reversal (RREDF) indicates severe placental resistance and is a precursor to fetal hypoxia and metabolic acidosis.

PubMed Clinical Insight: The TRUFFLE Trial

The TRUFFLE trial (Lancet, 2015) emphasized that in early-onset FGR, the Ductus Venosus (DV) Doppler is the best parameter for timing delivery to minimize neurological impairment. For late-onset FGR, the Cerebroplacental Ratio (CPR) is a more sensitive marker for detecting subtle placental failure.

3. Preeclampsia with Severe Features (PEC-SF)

Hypertensive disorders of pregnancy peak in the third trimester. Clinical management of PEC-SF requires the ASPHERE protocol: Blood pressure control (Labetalol/Hydralazine/Nifedipine) and seizure prophylaxis using **Magnesium Sulfate** (Pritchard or Zuspan regimen). The PG resident must monitor for signs of MgSO4 toxicity (loss of patellar reflex, RR < 12/min) and have Calcium Gluconate ready at the bedside.

⚠️ RED FLAG: Sudden onset RUQ pain, visual disturbances, or persistent headache in the 3rd trimester must be treated as impending eclampsia until proven otherwise.

4. Screening and Prophylaxis: GBS & Rh

At 36 0/7 to 37 6/7 weeks, universal screening for Group B Streptococcus (GBS) via recto-vaginal swab is mandatory (ACOG Level A). Positive status requires intrapartum antibiotic prophylaxis (IAP) with Penicillin G to prevent neonatal sepsis. For Rh-negative unsensitized patients, the second dose of Anti-D (300mcg) is administered at 28 weeks.

5. Assessment for Delivery: The Bishop Score

As the pregnancy reaches term (37-40 weeks), the specialist must evaluate the "readiness" of the cervix for labor induction. The **Bishop Score** remains the most reliable clinical tool for predicting the success of vaginal delivery.

6. Antepartum Hemorrhage (APH): Placenta Previa vs. Abruption

APH in the third trimester is a medical emergency. The postgraduate must distinguish between **Placenta Previa** (painless, bright red bleeding) and **Abruptio Placentae** (painful, dark bleeding, woody hard uterus). Digital vaginal examination is strictly CONTRAINDICATED until placenta previa is ruled out by USG.

7. Mode of Delivery and The "ACOG Safe Prevention"

In line with the ACOG/SMFM Joint Workshop on Safe Prevention of the Primary Cesarean Delivery, clinicians are encouraged to allow for a longer latent phase and use operative vaginal delivery (Forceps/Ventouse) when appropriate. However, immediate Cesarean section is indicated for maternal/fetal compromise or Category III CTG tracings.
