Vitamin D Supplementation and Sunlight Exposure: Effects on Serum Vitamin D Levels

Introduction

Vitamin D plays a crucial role in maintaining calcium balance, bone health, neuromuscular function, and metabolic processes. The biologically active form of vitamin D (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D) works alongside parathyroid hormone (PTH) to regulate calcium and phosphorus metabolism, enhance intestinal calcium absorption, and support bone formation.

Vitamin D status is influenced primarily by two sources:

  • Sunlight exposure (cutaneous synthesis via UVB radiation)
  • Dietary intake and supplementation

Up to 90% of vitamin D in humans may originate from skin exposure to sunlight. However, modern lifestyles, geographical differences, and seasonal variation often limit sufficient sunlight exposure.

Study Objective

This study aimed to investigate:

  1. The effect of vitamin D supplementation on serum vitamin D levels.
  2. The role of sunlight exposure in maintaining vitamin D status.
  3. Whether latitude (high vs low) influences vitamin D status when supplementation is provided.

Study Design

The study consisted of two parallel double-blind randomized placebo-controlled trials.

Population: Brazilian women, Age: 20–59 years, Total participants analyzed: 135.

Location Latitude Sunlight Availability
England 51°N Low UVB in winter
Brazil 16°S High UVB exposure

Intervention

Participants were randomized into four groups over a duration of 12 weeks (winter period) with a supplement dose of 15 μg/day Vitamin D3 (Equivalent to 600 IU/day).

Baseline Findings

At baseline, women living in Brazil had much higher vitamin D levels than those in England due to greater UVB exposure.

Group Mean Vitamin D Level
England~35–38 nmol/L
Brazil~74–75 nmol/L

Effect of Vitamin D Supplementation

Vitamin D supplementation significantly increased serum vitamin D levels, independent of sunlight exposure.

Key Conclusions

  1. Moderate Supplementation Works: Daily dose of 15 μg vitamin D3 effectively maintains adequate levels.
  2. Latitude Independence: Supplementation improves status regardless of geographic location.
  3. Baseline Importance: Baseline levels are the strongest predictor of final status.
  4. Sunlight Insufficiency: Even in sunny regions, supplementation may be necessary due to indoor lifestyles.

Clinical Implications

This study supports public health recommendations that adults should consume around 15 μg (600 IU) vitamin D daily, particularly during winter months or in high-latitude regions.
