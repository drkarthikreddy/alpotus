What is the CURB-65 Score?

The CURB-65 score is a validated clinical prediction rule used to assess the severity of Community-Acquired Pneumonia (CAP). Developed by the British Thoracic Society and popularized in the early 2000s, it provides clinicians with an objective framework to decide whether a patient can be safely treated at home or requires hospitalization. The acronym represents five core physiological variables: Confusion, Urea, Respiratory rate, Blood pressure, and Age.

How to Use the Alpotus CURB-65 Engine

To use this calculator, perform a clinical assessment of the patient and enter the laboratory and vital signs. A point is assigned for each of the following criteria:

  • C (Confusion): Use a validated mental test or observe new disorientation.
  • U (Urea): Blood Urea Nitrogen (BUN) > 19 mg/dL (7 mmol/L). Note: Our tool allows you to toggle between units for global clinical use.
  • R (Respiratory Rate): ≥ 30 breaths per minute.
  • B (Blood Pressure): Systolic < 90 mmHg or Diastolic ≤ 60 mmHg.
  • 65: Age 65 or older.

Significance and Clinical Triage

The significance of the CURB-65 score lies in its ability to streamline triage in high-pressure emergency environments. Research shows that patients with a score of 0 or 1 have a very low risk of mortality (less than 2%) and are often suitable for outpatient management. Conversely, a score of 3 or higher indicates a medical emergency with a mortality risk reaching 22%, necessitating urgent inpatient care or ICU admission.

PubMed Studies and Clinical Evidence

Clinical Study Highlight (Lim et al., 2003): A landmark study published in Thorax (PubMed ID: 12728160) involved a derivation cohort of 1,068 patients. It confirmed that CURB-65 effectively stratifies patients into low, moderate, and high-risk groups for death within 30 days.

Comparison to PSI (Pneumonia Severity Index)

While the Pneumonia Severity Index (PSI) is also widely used, CURB-65 is preferred in many emergency departments due to its simplicity. A comparative study in The Lancet suggested that while PSI is slightly more accurate at identifying low-risk patients, CURB-65 is far more practical for rapid decision-making because it requires fewer variables and no complex laboratory calculations.
