What is the MELD Score?

The Model for End-Stage Liver Disease (MELD) is a validated numerical scale used to estimate the severity of chronic liver disease. Originally developed at the Mayo Clinic to predict survival in patients undergoing Transjugular Intrahepatic Portosystemic Shunt (TIPS) procedures, it was adopted by the United States Organ Procurement and Transplantation Network (OPTN) in 2002 to prioritize patients on the liver transplant waiting list. Unlike the older Child-Pugh system, the MELD score uses objective laboratory variables, reducing clinical bias.

How to Use the MELD-Na Calculator

To calculate the score, four laboratory values are required: Total Bilirubin, Serum Creatinine, INR, and Serum Sodium. If a patient has undergone hemodialysis at least twice within the week prior to the blood draw, the creatinine value is automatically capped at 4.0 mg/dL for clinical safety. The Alpotus engine applies these clinical rules (including rounding values less than 1.0 up to 1.0) to provide the most accurate transplant-priority score.

Significance of MELD-Na

Since 2016, Sodium (Na+) has been integrated into the standard MELD formula. Clinical research proved that hyponatremia (low sodium) is an independent and powerful predictor of mortality in patients with cirrhosis. By accounting for sodium, the MELD-Na score prevents the underestimation of risk in patients with portal hypertension and fluid retention issues.

Clinical Mortality Correlation

The MELD score is primarily used to predict 3-month mortality. Generally, a higher score indicates a more urgent need for a liver transplant. Scores range from 6 to 40:

  • MELD < 10: ~1.9% mortality risk.
  • MELD 20–29: ~19.6% mortality risk.
  • MELD 30–39: ~52.6% mortality risk.
  • MELD ≥ 40: ~71.3% mortality risk.
