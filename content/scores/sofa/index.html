What is the SOFA Score?

The Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score is a clinical mortality prediction tool used to track a patient's status during their stay in an intensive care unit (ICU). Originally known as the Sepsis-related Organ Failure Assessment score, it was developed by a task force from the European Society of Intensive Care Medicine in 1994. Its primary utility lies in its ability to quantify the degree of dysfunction across six organ systems: respiratory, cardiovascular, hepatic, coagulation, renal, and neurological.

Significance and Clinical Use

In modern medicine, the SOFA score gained renewed global significance with the release of the **Sepsis-3 consensus definitions** in 2016. According to the Third International Consensus Definitions for Sepsis and Septic Shock, sepsis is now defined as a life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection, clinically characterized by an increase in the SOFA score of **2 points or more** from the patient's baseline.

"The Sequential Organ Failure Assessment (SOFA) score is more than a one-time calculation; it is a dynamic monitoring tool. Trends in SOFA scores over the first 48 hours of admission are highly predictive of a patient's ultimate prognosis."

How to Use the Alpotus SOFA Engine

To perform a comprehensive assessment, enter the worst values recorded within the last 24 hours for each system:

  • Respiration: Based on the PaO2/FiO2 ratio. If the patient is on mechanical ventilation, higher scores are assigned for lower ratios.
  • Coagulation: Evaluated using the platelet count. Thrombocytopenia is a hallmark of severe sepsis and DIC.
  • Liver: Measured by serum bilirubin levels.
  • Cardiovascular: Assesses hypotension and the requirement for vasopressors (Dopamine, Epinephrine, Norepinephrine) to maintain MAP.
  • CNS: Utilizes the Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS).
  • Renal: Measured by serum creatinine or daily urine output.

PubMed Studies and Clinical Evidence

The validity of the SOFA score is supported by decades of multi-center clinical research. A landmark study published in JAMA (PubMed ID: 26903338) demonstrated that for patients with suspected infection, the predictive validity of the SOFA score for in-hospital mortality was significantly superior to that of the SIRS criteria.

Further research published in the Critical Care Medicine journal indicates that the change in SOFA score (Delta-SOFA) during the first week of ICU stay is an independent predictor of survival. Patients whose scores increase by more than 2 points have a mortality rate exceeding 50%, whereas those with decreasing scores show significantly improved outcomes.
